# Artificial Intelligence and the Australian Labour Market: A Policy Proposal

**To:** Deputy Secretary, Workforce Policy and Innovation, Department of Employment and Workplace Relations
**From:** Maria AlGhamdi
**Date:** 10 May 2026
**Re:** Managing AI's Impact on the Australian Labour Market

---

## Executive Summary

Artificial intelligence is reshaping work across every major sector of the Australian economy, and this affects not just which jobs exist but how tasks within those jobs are performed, how wages are set, and how industries are structured. This proposal analyses the key channels through which AI is transforming the Australian labour market, with reference to sectors including healthcare and aged care, finance, retail, logistics and customer service. It evaluates three policy options and recommends a coordinated national response built around workforce reskilling, targeted support for displaced workers, and an ethical governance framework for AI deployment in the workplace. Without deliberate government action, AI's productivity gains are likely to be unevenly distributed, disproportionately burdening low-wage workers, women and regional Australians while rewarding those already at the top of the labour market.

---

## Problem Definition and Context

AI adoption in Australia is accelerating rapidly. The AI market in Australian healthcare alone was valued at $197.6 million in 2023 and is projected to reach $2.16 billion by 2030 (TechNew 2025). Across the broader economy, generative AI tools are being integrated into professional services, logistics operations, customer service functions and administrative roles at a pace that existing policy frameworks were not designed to manage.

The Australian labour market is already under structural pressure. Health Care and Social Assistance is the country's largest employing industry, projected to need 3 million workers by May 2034 (Absolute Immigration Legal 2025). At the same time, automation is threatening routine task-intensive roles in retail, finance and logistics. The challenge for government is not simply whether AI creates or destroys jobs in aggregate, but how the transition is managed and who bears the costs of adjustment.

## Analysis of Impacts

### Employment and Task Change

The economic literature draws an important distinction between job-level displacement and task-level automation. Acemoglu and Restrepo (2022) demonstrate that AI and automation primarily affect task bundles within occupations rather than eliminating whole jobs at once. This distinction matters enormously for policy design as seen in the argument above. 

In healthcare and aged care, AI is automating administrative tasks such as rostering, documentation and medication reminders, while augmenting clinical roles rather than replacing them. Australia's aged care sector employs approximately 456,000 workers and faces a projected shortfall of at least 110,000 direct care workers by 2030 (CEDA 2025). Here, AI tools help stretch an insufficient workforce further rather than reduce it.

In contrast, customer service and retail are experiencing more direct displacement. Chatbots and automated checkout systems are reducing demand for routine inquiry handlers and checkout operators. In finance, AI is automating fraud detection, credit assessment and data processing, which affects mid-level analytical roles. In logistics, route optimisation and warehouse automation are changing the task composition of transport and distribution work, reducing demand for some manual handling roles while creating new demand for workers who can manage and maintain automated systems.

The net employment effect across sectors is contested. While AI creates new roles in technology development, model oversight and digital operations, these typically require higher qualifications than the roles being displaced, meaning the transition is not smooth or automatic for affected workers.

### Wages and Distributional Effects

AI's effect on wages is heterogeneous and likely to widen existing inequalities. Workers whose tasks are complemented by AI tools tend to see productivity gains that can translate into higher wages. Workers whose tasks are substituted by AI face wage pressure or displacement. Brynjolfsson and McAfee (2014) describe this dynamic as a key driver of the "great decoupling" between productivity growth and median wage growth in advanced economies.

In Australia, this risk is concentrated among workers in routine, task-intensive occupations. The aged and disabled carer workforce has a median full-time weekly earnings figure of approximately $1,564 and is 76% female (Absolute Immigration Legal 2025). These workers are simultaneously among those most likely to be affected by AI-driven restructuring and among those least equipped, through financial resources or flexible work arrangements, to undertake retraining. Regional and remote workers face an additional disadvantage, given that digital infrastructure gaps limit access to both AI-enabled services and online training programs.

### Industry Structure

AI adoption is also reshaping the competitive structure of Australian industries. In sectors where AI tools deliver significant efficiency gains, large firms with the capital to invest in technology early gain advantages over smaller competitors. In aged care, KPMG (2026) reports that there are now 873 home care providers and 636 residential aged care providers nationally, and ongoing reforms are expected to further consolidate the market. Smaller regional providers, which lack the scale to absorb upfront technology costs, risk being squeezed out, reducing service access in already underserviced communities.

A similar dynamic is visible in retail and logistics, where major platforms and supermarket chains are investing heavily in automation while independent operators cannot match the capital expenditure. Government policy needs to account for how AI-driven market concentration interacts with labour market outcomes, particularly in sectors where geographic coverage matters for community wellbeing.

## Policy Options

| Criterion | Option 1: National Reskilling Fund | Option 2: AI Workplace Standards | Option 3: Integrated AI and Work Strategy |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Description** | Competitive grants to fund worker retraining in AI-exposed industries | Legislate minimum transparency and consultation requirements for AI deployment by employers | Combine reskilling investment, equity-weighted transition support, and a governance framework for AI at work |
| **Efficiency** | High in the short term; does not address governance gaps | Medium; reduces harm but does not build capacity | High; addresses demand and supply side simultaneously |
| **Equity** | Medium; market-led uptake may favour larger employers | Medium; applies uniformly but does not fund retraining | High; explicit weighting for low-wage, regional and female workers |
| **Feasibility** | High; builds on existing TAFE and VET infrastructure | Medium; requires new legislative framework | Medium; requires cross-departmental coordination |
| **Evidence base** | Strong; consistent with OECD active labour market policy evidence | Moderate; comparable to WHS consultation frameworks | Strong; aligns with CEDA (2025), Acemoglu and Restrepo (2022), and OECD recommendations |
| **Key risk** | Demand for retraining outpaces supply of qualified trainers | Compliance costs may slow beneficial AI adoption | Complex to administer across multiple agencies |

Option 1 addresses the skills mismatch but does not manage how AI is introduced in workplaces, leaving workers exposed to arbitrary displacement without consultation or recourse.

Option 2 improves transparency and worker voice in AI adoption decisions but without investment in reskilling, workers remain unable to transition to new roles even if they are consulted about change.

Option 3 is the recommended approach. It treats the reskilling challenge and the governance challenge as inseparable, and includes equity provisions that the other two options lack.

## Recommended Package and Implementation Plan

This proposal recommends Option 3, an Integrated AI and Work Strategy, delivered in three phases.

**Phase 1: Foundation (2026-2027, estimated cost $150 million)**
The Commonwealth establishes a National AI and Work Taskforce, co-chaired by the Department of Employment and Workplace Relations and the Department of Industry, Science and Resources. The Taskforce commissions a national labour market exposure assessment, mapping which occupational task bundles across all industries are most vulnerable to automation in the next five years. This evidence base will underpin all subsequent investment decisions. Responsibility: Department of Employment and Workplace Relations, Jobs and Skills Australia. KPI: exposure assessment published by December 30th 2026, covering all two-digit ANZSCO occupation groups.

**Phase 2: Reskilling and Transition Support (2027-2029, estimated cost $320 million)**
A national AI Transition Fund is established within the existing VET funding framework, providing fee-free short courses in digital literacy, AI tool operation and adjacent skills for workers in high-exposure occupations. Forty per cent of funding is equity-weighted toward low-wage workers, women, and workers in regional and remote areas. Employers who deploy AI tools that result in measurable task displacement are required to contribute to the Fund through a levy, internalising the cost of transition rather than externalising it to workers and the public. Responsibility: Department of Employment and Workplace Relations, TAFE Australia, Fair Work Commission. KPI: 80,000 workers complete an accredited AI transition qualification by 2029; no net reduction in employment levels in targeted high-exposure occupational groups.

**Phase 3: Governance and Review (2029 onwards)**
The Fair Work Act is amended to require employers to consult with workers and their representatives before deploying AI systems that materially alter task composition or supervision arrangements. An annual National AI and Labour Market Report is published by Jobs and Skills Australia, tracking wage, employment and industry concentration effects of AI adoption across sectors. An independent five-year review assesses distributional outcomes and recommends adjustments to the Fund and governance framework. Responsibility: Fair Work Commission, Jobs and Skills Australia, Productivity Commission. KPI: 100% of employers with more than 50 staff have documented AI consultation processes in place by 2030.

## Risks and Mitigations

**Risk 1: Retraining supply cannot keep pace with demand.** The HIC 2024 seminar found that 66% of healthcare leaders cited staff readiness as their biggest barrier to AI adoption (TechNew 2025). The same constraint applies to retraining delivery. Mitigation: seed funding for TAFE AI curriculum development from Phase 1, and allow recognition of prior learning so experienced workers can fast-track qualifications.

**Risk 2: Employer levy drives reluctance to invest in AI domestically.** Mitigation: cap the levy at a percentage of documented productivity savings attributable to AI deployment, assessed through existing Fair Work Commission mechanisms, so it does not create a disincentive for beneficial adoption.

**Risk 3: Regional providers and workers are left behind.** Mitigation: digital infrastructure investment for regional areas is designated a prerequisite for Phase 2 funding, coordinated with the existing Regional Connectivity Program.
